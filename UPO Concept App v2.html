01Executive Vision

From passive recipient to operator: the logic of Out-In

Move from an outsourced model to in-house production and distribution, building operational capacity in the 11 centers before jumping to UPO's own central kitchen.

a

Starting point — UPO only receives and reheats

Today UPO is the endpoint of a chain it does not control: it receives meals from Jeffrey's Catering and reheats them on site. No control over recipes, seasonal adjustments, or cold chain.

This passive position caps service quality, regulatory traceability, and UPO's ability to integrate food with its educational mission.

Outsourced No chain control No traceability
b

Out-In approach — centers first, kitchen second

Build operational capacity in the 11 receiving centers before jumping to large-scale in-house production. Standardize intake, sanitation, transport, and compliance at the points where food arrives and is served.

Why Out-In
Reduces implementation risk, creates the regulatory foundation for the future central kitchen, and decouples infrastructure investment from culture and process work.
c

Destination — social enterprise with central kitchen

A UPO-operated central kitchen that, at maturity, serves not only its own centers but other D.C. organizations — schools, community programs, after-school sites.

The social enterprise component is central: third-party revenue partially funds operations, reducing grant dependence.

Social enterprise Third-party Revenue diversification
02Model Evaluation

Executive balance: advantages vs. risks

Four reasons to bring catering in-house and four risks the roadmap must explicitly mitigate.

Advantages Pros

Why in-house unlocks value UPO cannot get from a contract.

01

Full quality control

UPO sets recipes, makes seasonal adjustments with local product, and maintains verifiable control over the cold chain end-to-end.

02

Mission alignment & integration

Food becomes part of the educational value and creates real opportunities for UPO's culinary students — internships, employment, mentorship.

03

Scalability with growth

Capacity scales by planned increments: 12 centers in 2027, 14 in 2028. An external contract scales by renegotiation.

04

Revenue · social enterprise

Once stable, the kitchen sells to other D.C. organizations and generates revenue that reduces grant dependence.

Risks Cons

What this roadmap must mitigate from day one.

01

Significant CapEx

Commercial kitchen equipment, specialized hires, distribution logistics, certifications. The roadmap must stage the investment.

02

Zero margin for error

Any failure directly impacts children's meals. The design must include redundancies, contingency plans, and a backup vendor.

03

Regulatory load

UPO assumes full compliance with DC Health, USDA, CACFP: logs, labeling, allergens, age portions, cold chain, audits.

04

Distraction from core mission

Food service must have its own chain of command and not cannibalize UPO's educational team.

04Action Plan · Phase 1

Six workstreams to ready the 11 centers

Concrete initiatives, executable without infrastructure CapEx. Each one protects the financial model or builds regulatory ground.

A

Modernize and automate the intake process

Today
Friday MS Forms → Monday Excel compile. Amazon milks on a separate, disconnected flow.
Proposed
Single unified form. Power Automate generates two POs automatically on Friday.
Value
Clean forecasting from day one. When UPO cooks in-house, demand data is already projectable.
B

Waste traceability and menu optimization

Today
Rejected components go to trash; UPO still pays. No structured feedback or recipe adjustment.
Proposed
Weekly feedback loop. Contractually require recipe adjustments based on data.
Value
Protects the $4.60 / lunch · CACFP free tier model and builds the historical record for an internal menu kids actually eat.
C

Food-safety equipment

  • Thermometers. Digital probe units + alcohol wipes; ambient thermometers in all cold storage.
  • Tables & utensils. Commercial stainless steel; portion scoops for age-standardized servings.
  • Refrigeration. Replace household units with commercial foodservice equipment.
Value
Closes typical DC Health inspection findings before they happen. Equipment baseline meets ServSafe.
D

Receiving and transport logistics

Today
Burned foam Cambros stored on the floor with cardboard inserts. Severe cross-contamination risk.
Proposed
Easy-to-clean commercial containers. Prohibit cardboard and floor storage. Documented protocol.
Value
Eliminates a visible regulatory risk and sets the transport standard the central kitchen will use.
E

Sanitation where infrastructure is missing

Today
Several locations lack 3-compartment sinks. Sanitation improvised or skipped.
Proposed
Red sanitation buckets + chlorine test kits. Target 50–100 ppm, verifiable.
Value
Low-cost interim that meets compliance until proper infrastructure exists — without pausing operations.
F

Immediate hire — CACFP Food Service Program Manager

Key decision
Do NOT hire an Executive Chef yet. UPO needs a compliance manager, not a chef.

Role profile

Bilingual EN/ES ServSafe Manager Formal CACFP training
  • Single point of contact with Jeffrey's Catering.
  • Weekly rotating audits across the 11 centers: cold chain, age portions, allergens, milk labeling.
  • Owns regulatory documentation: logs, headcounts, allergens, CACFP reporting.
  • Recurring staff training — onboarding and quarterly refresh.
Value
Builds the entire regulatory base the future central kitchen will inherit. Highest-leverage human investment in Phase 1.
